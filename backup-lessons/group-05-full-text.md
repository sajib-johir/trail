## 121. Aloof

**Pronunciation:** ə-LOOF
**Part of speech:** Adjective
**Core meaning:** Distant, reserved, or emotionally detached.
**Bangla meaning:** দূরবর্তী, বিচ্ছিন্ন, উদাসীন, গম্ভীরভাবে আলাদা থাকা

**Root story / etymology idea:**
*Aloof* likely came from old nautical language, connected with keeping a ship “to windward” or away from something. The original image is a ship staying apart from others. So, mentally: **aloof = keeping emotional distance.**

**Simple explanation:**
An **aloof** person does not easily mix with others. They may seem cold, distant, or uninterested.

**Memory hook:**
**Aloof = alone + off** → someone stays “off” from the group.

**GRE synonyms:** distant, detached, remote, reserved, withdrawn, standoffish
**Basic synonyms:** cold, unfriendly, separate, not close
**Antonyms:** friendly, sociable, warm, approachable, involved

**Common usage:** aloof professor, aloof attitude, aloof manner, emotionally aloof, remain aloof from politics

**Confusing word:**
**Aloof ≠ Alone**
Alone = physically by oneself.
Aloof = emotionally or socially distant.

### Examples — 10

1. The new student seemed **aloof** during lunch.
2. The manager was polite but **aloof**.
3. She stayed **aloof** from office gossip.
4. The professor’s **aloof** manner made students hesitant to ask questions.
5. Even after joining the committee, he remained **aloof** from its internal debates.
6. The CEO’s **aloof** communication style created distance between leadership and employees.
7. In academic discussions, an **aloof** tone may appear objective but can also seem dismissive.
8. The diplomat remained **aloof** from the controversy to avoid appearing biased.
9. Although the scientist was brilliant, his **aloof** personality limited collaboration.
10. GRE-style: The candidate’s **aloof** demeanor, though interpreted by some as arrogance, was actually a defense against public scrutiny.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. The witness gave a **detached** account of the accident.
2. The judge remained **impartial** and emotionally **detached**.
3. The remote village was geographically **isolated** from major cities.
4. His **reserved** personality made him difficult to know.
5. The critic’s **distant** tone suggested little sympathy for the artist.
6. She was **withdrawn** after the argument.
7. The professor seemed **standoffish** at first, but later proved helpful.
8. The committee adopted a **neutral** position during the dispute.
9. His **remote** expression revealed no emotion.
10. The aristocrat’s **haughty** reserve offended the guests.
11. The analyst maintained a **dispassionate** perspective.
12. The director was **unapproachable** during the crisis.
13. The scholar’s **impersonal** writing style suited the scientific topic.
14. The leader appeared **cold** toward public criticism.
15. The official’s **detachment** helped him avoid emotional decision-making.

**Common mistake:**
Do not use **aloof** only for physical distance. It usually means emotional or social distance.

**Final memory:**
**Aloof = emotionally distant = দূরত্ব বজায় রাখা**

## 122. Clangor

**Pronunciation:** KLANG-gər
**Part of speech:** Noun
**Core meaning:** A loud, harsh, repeated noise.
**Bangla meaning:** কোলাহল, কর্কশ শব্দ, উচ্চ ধাতব আওয়াজ

**Root story / etymology idea:**
*Clangor* comes from Latin *clangere*, meaning “to resound” or “to make a loud metallic sound.” Think of metal hitting metal.

**Simple explanation:**
**Clangor** is not soft sound. It is loud, unpleasant, noisy, and often metallic.

**Memory hook:**
**Clangor = clang + noise** → metal doors banging loudly.

**GRE synonyms:** clamor, racket, uproar, din, cacophony, tumult
**Basic synonyms:** loud noise, noise, shouting, crash
**Antonyms:** silence, quiet, calm, hush, stillness

**Common usage:** clangor of bells, clangor of machinery, industrial clangor, street clangor

**Confusing word:**
**Clangor ≠ Clamor**
Both mean loud noise, but **clamor** can also mean loud public demand.
Example: public clamor for reform.

### Examples — 10

1. The **clangor** of the bells woke everyone.
2. We heard the **clangor** of metal gates.
3. The factory was filled with the **clangor** of machinery.
4. The **clangor** from the construction site made reading impossible.
5. The morning market began with the **clangor** of carts, vendors, and traffic.
6. The **clangor** of political slogans drowned out the speaker.
7. In urban studies, noise pollution often includes the daily **clangor** of transportation.
8. The poem contrasts rural silence with the industrial **clangor** of the city.
9. The laboratory’s calm atmosphere was suddenly broken by the **clangor** of falling equipment.
10. GRE-style: The reformer’s quiet proposal was lost amid the **clangor** of partisan accusations.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. The **clamor** of the crowd filled the stadium.
2. Public **clamor** forced the minister to respond.
3. The children created a terrible **racket** upstairs.
4. The political rally descended into **uproar**.
5. The city’s morning **din** exhausted the villagers.
6. The orchestra produced a painful **cacophony** during rehearsal.
7. The meeting ended in **tumult**.
8. The machines **resounded** through the workshop.
9. The alarm bells **clanged** throughout the building.
10. The marketplace was full of **commotion**.
11. The protesters’ **outcry** reached parliament.
12. The crash produced a metallic **clatter**.
13. The announcement caused immediate **uproar**.
14. The school hallway echoed with **noise** after the bell.
15. The factory floor was dominated by mechanical **clamor**.

**Common mistake:**
Do not use **clangor** for pleasant music. It usually suggests harsh, loud, unpleasant sound.

**Final memory:**
**Clangor = harsh loud noise = কর্কশ কোলাহল**

## 123. Conventional

**Pronunciation:** kən-VEN-shə-nəl
**Part of speech:** Adjective
**Core meaning:** Following accepted rules, customs, or standard ways.
**Bangla meaning:** প্রচলিত, প্রথাগত, সাধারণ নিয়মমাফিক

**Root story / etymology idea:**
*Conventional* comes from Latin *convenire*, meaning “to come together” or “to agree.” A convention is something people agree to follow.

**Simple explanation:**
Something **conventional** is normal, standard, traditional, or widely accepted.

**Memory hook:**
**Convention = social agreement** → conventional = what society agrees is normal.

**GRE synonyms:** traditional, customary, orthodox, standard, established
**Basic synonyms:** usual, normal, common, regular
**Antonyms:** unconventional, original, unusual, radical, innovative

**Common usage:** conventional wisdom, conventional medicine, conventional method, conventional style, conventional morality

**Confusing word:**
**Conventional ≠ Convenient**
Conventional = traditional/standard.
Convenient = easy or suitable.

### Examples — 10

1. They followed a **conventional** wedding style.
2. His clothes were simple and **conventional**.
3. The teacher used a **conventional** method.
4. Many patients first try **conventional** medicine before alternative treatment.
5. The architect rejected **conventional** designs and proposed a radical structure.
6. The company relied on **conventional** advertising rather than digital campaigns.
7. In academic writing, **conventional** arguments often need fresh evidence.
8. The study challenged **conventional** assumptions about economic growth.
9. The researcher used **conventional** statistical tools before applying machine learning.
10. GRE-style: Although the theory appeared **conventional**, its implications were surprisingly disruptive.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. The village followed **traditional** customs.
2. The lawyer used a **standard** contract.
3. The ceremony followed **customary** rules.
4. His beliefs were deeply **orthodox**.
5. The scientist questioned the **established** model.
6. The school preferred **regular** classroom instruction.
7. The committee accepted the **usual** procedure.
8. The company used **mainstream** marketing strategies.
9. The report reflected **accepted** practice.
10. The priest defended **orthodox** doctrine.
11. The professor criticized **standardized** thinking.
12. The policy followed **normal** administrative procedure.
13. The court relied on **precedent-based** reasoning.
14. The essay repeated **commonplace** views.
15. The engineer selected a **proven** design method.

**Common mistake:**
Do not confuse **conventional** with **boring** every time. Something conventional may be ordinary, but not always bad.

**Final memory:**
**Conventional = accepted standard = প্রচলিত**

## 124. Debunk

**Pronunciation:** dee-BUNK
**Part of speech:** Verb
**Core meaning:** To expose an idea, claim, or belief as false.
**Bangla meaning:** ভুল প্রমাণ করা, খণ্ডন করা, মিথ্যা উন্মোচন করা

**Root story / etymology idea:**
*Debunk* = *de-* “remove” + *bunk* “nonsense.” So debunk literally feels like **remove nonsense**.

**Simple explanation:**
To **debunk** something means to show evidence that it is false.

**Memory hook:**
**Debunk = delete bunk/nonsense.**

**GRE synonyms:** expose, disprove, discredit, refute, invalidate
**Basic synonyms:** prove wrong, show false, challenge, correct
**Antonyms:** confirm, support, validate, verify, prove

**Common usage:** debunk a myth, debunk a theory, debunk a rumor, debunk misinformation

**Confusing word:**
**Debunk ≠ Debut**
Debunk = prove false.
Debut = first public appearance.

### Examples — 10

1. The article **debunked** the rumor.
2. Scientists **debunked** the fake cure.
3. The teacher **debunked** a common myth.
4. The documentary **debunked** several conspiracy theories.
5. The new evidence **debunked** the company’s misleading safety claim.
6. Researchers **debunked** the belief that the method worked in all cases.
7. In public health, experts often **debunk** misinformation about vaccines.
8. The historian **debunked** the romanticized version of the event.
9. The paper **debunked** earlier assumptions by using a larger dataset.
10. GRE-style: The critic did not merely question the legend; he **debunked** it with archival evidence.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. The experiment **disproved** the old theory.
2. The witness helped **refute** the accusation.
3. The leaked documents **exposed** the fraud.
4. The scandal **discredited** the official report.
5. The data **invalidated** the initial hypothesis.
6. The article **challenged** popular assumptions.
7. The investigation **unmasked** the deception.
8. The scientist **contradicted** the earlier findings.
9. The lawyer **undermined** the witness’s credibility.
10. The study **corrected** a widespread misconception.
11. The audit **revealed** accounting irregularities.
12. The new test **falsified** the claim.
13. The report **dismissed** the rumor as baseless.
14. The evidence **overturned** the accepted explanation.
15. The scholar **punctured** the myth of national unity.

**Common mistake:**
Use **debunk** for claims, myths, rumors, theories, or misinformation—not for physically destroying something.

**Final memory:**
**Debunk = prove false = ভুল প্রমাণ করা**

**Common mistake:**

**Final memory:**
**Diminutive = very small = ক্ষুদ্র**

## 126. Discernible

**Pronunciation:** dih-SUR-nuh-bəl
**Part of speech:** Adjective
**Core meaning:** Able to be seen, noticed, or understood.
**Bangla meaning:** দৃশ্যমান, বোঝা যায় এমন, শনাক্তযোগ্য

**Root story / etymology idea:**
*Discernible* comes from Latin *discernere*, meaning “to separate, distinguish, perceive.” If something is discernible, your mind can separate it from the background.

**Simple explanation:**
If a change is **discernible**, you can notice it.

**Memory hook:**
**Discern = detect** → discernible = detectable.

**GRE synonyms:** detectable, noticeable, observable, perceptible, apparent
**Basic synonyms:** visible, clear, seen, noticeable
**Antonyms:** invisible, hidden, indistinct, imperceptible, obscure

**Common usage:** discernible change, discernible pattern, discernible difference, barely discernible

**Confusing word:**
**Discernible ≠ Discerning**
Discernible = able to be noticed.
Discerning = having good judgment.

### Examples — 10

1. There was a **discernible** smell of smoke.
2. The difference was barely **discernible**.
3. A **discernible** pattern appeared in the data.
4. The medicine produced a **discernible** improvement after two weeks.
5. The speaker’s nervousness was **discernible** in his voice.
6. The policy had no **discernible** effect on unemployment.
7. In research, a **discernible** correlation does not always prove causation.
8. The satellite image showed a **discernible** change in coastline shape.
9. The essay lacked a **discernible** thesis, making its argument difficult to follow.
10. GRE-style: Although the reform was praised, its benefits were scarcely **discernible** in the poorest districts.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. A **detectable** rise in temperature worried the engineers.
2. The error was **noticeable** even to beginners.
3. The trend became **observable** after several trials.
4. The difference was **perceptible** only under magnification.
5. His frustration was **apparent**.
6. The pattern was **visible** in the graph.
7. The signal was **measurable** but weak.
8. The damage was **evident** after inspection.
9. The shift in tone was **clear**.
10. The symptoms were **identifiable** by trained doctors.
11. The improvement was **marked** after the intervention.
12. The bias was **manifest** in the report.
13. The contrast was **plain** to observers.
14. The change was **recognizable** across all samples.
15. The effect became **salient** in the final analysis.

**Common mistake:**
Do not use **discernible** when nothing can be noticed. Say “not discernible” or “barely discernible.”

**Final memory:**
**Discernible = noticeable = বোঝা যায় এমন**

## 129. Extravagant

*Extravagant* comes from Latin ideas meaning “wandering outside bounds.” Something extravagant goes beyond normal limits.

An **extravagant** person spends too much. An extravagant plan may be too excessive.

**Extravagant = beyond reasonable limits = অমিতব্যয়ী / অতিরিক্ত**

**Bangla meaning:** উৎসাহী, আগ্রহী, প্রবলভাবে নিবেদিত, উত্সর্গীকৃত

**Root story / etymology idea:**
**Zealous** comes from Greek *zelos*, meaning zeal, passionate enthusiasm, or eager rivalry. It suggests strong devotion.

**Simple explanation:**
A **zealous** person works passionately for a belief, cause, duty, or goal.

**Memory hook:**
**Zealous = full of zeal.**

**GRE synonyms:** enthusiastic, passionate, fervent, ardent, devoted
**Basic synonyms:** eager, committed, active, energetic
**Antonyms:** apathetic, indifferent, lazy, unenthusiastic, halfhearted

**Common usage:** zealous activist, zealous supporter, zealous reformer, zealous effort

**Confusing word:**
**Zealous ≠ Jealous**
Zealous = passionate and devoted.
Jealous = envious or possessive.

### Examples — 10

1. She is a **zealous** student.
2. The volunteer was **zealous** about helping others.
3. He became a **zealous** supporter of the reform.
4. The **zealous** activist worked day and night.
5. The teacher’s **zealous** commitment inspired students.
6. The scientist was **zealous** in defending ethical research practices.
7. In politics, **zealous** supporters can energize a campaign.
8. The reformer’s **zealous** pursuit of justice impressed even critics.
9. The organization relied on **zealous** volunteers during the emergency.
10. GRE-style: His **zealous** advocacy of innovation sometimes made him impatient with cautious colleagues.

### GRE Synonym Examples — 15

1. The coach was **enthusiastic** about the team’s progress.
2. The artist was **passionate** about traditional music.
3. The preacher gave a **fervent** speech.
4. The reformer was **ardent** in his support of equality.
5. The nurse was **devoted** to patient care.
6. The student was **eager** to learn.
7. The activist showed **intense** commitment.
8. The researcher was **dedicated** to the project.
9. The campaign attracted **fervid** supporters.
10. The lawyer was **committed** to defending civil rights.
11. The volunteer worked with **earnest** energy.
12. The scholar was **impassioned** in debate.
13. The group showed **wholehearted** support.
14. The environmentalist was **ardent** about conservation.
15. The teacher’s **enthusiasm** made difficult topics enjoyable.

**Common mistake:**
**Zealous** can be positive, but too much zeal may become extreme or intolerant.

**Final memory:**
**Zealous = passionately devoted = প্রবল উৎসাহী**
