# 31. Adulterate

**Pronunciation:** uh-DUL-tuh-rayt
**Part of speech:** Verb
**Core meaning:** To make something impure or worse by adding something bad or inferior.
**Bangla meaning:** ভেজাল মেশানো, বিশুদ্ধতা নষ্ট করা, নিম্নমানের করা

**Root story / etymology:** From Latin *adulterare*, meaning “to corrupt, pollute, or falsify.” The original idea is making something less pure than it was.

**Simple explanation:** If someone adulterates milk, oil, medicine, food, or data, they mix in something bad or false, so the original thing loses quality.

**Memory hook:** **Adulterate = add dirt / add lower-quality material.**

**GRE synonyms:** contaminate, debase, corrupt, taint, pollute, dilute
**Basic synonyms:** mix badly, make impure, spoil, lower quality
**Antonyms:** purify, refine, cleanse, improve
**Common usage:** adulterated food, adulterated milk, adulterated data, adulterated evidence
**Confusing word:** **Adulterate ≠ Adultery.** Adulterate = ভেজাল মেশানো; adultery = marital unfaithfulness.

### Examples — 10

1. The seller adulterated the milk with water.
2. Cheap oil can adulterate good-quality fuel.
3. The company was fined for selling adulterated spices.
4. The researcher warned that biased sampling could adulterate the results.
5. If the evidence is adulterated, the court cannot trust it.
6. The drug lost effectiveness after being adulterated with inactive chemicals.
7. A single false entry can adulterate an entire database.
8. In public health studies, adulterated medicine is a serious threat to patient safety.
9. In food-quality research, laboratories test whether imported honey has been adulterated with sugar syrup.
10. The integrity of the experiment was compromised because the sample had been adulterated before analysis.

### GRE synonym examples — 15

1. The river was **contaminated** by industrial waste.
2. False data can **contaminate** a scientific model.
3. The dictator’s greed **debased** public institutions.
4. Plagiarism can **debase** an academic paper.
5. The official tried to **corrupt** the investigation.
6. Rumors can **taint** a person’s reputation.
7. The scandal **tainted** the minister’s career.
8. Factories may **pollute** groundwater with untreated waste.
9. Propaganda can **pollute** public debate.
10. Adding weak evidence may **dilute** a strong argument.
11. Overuse of clichés can **dilute** the force of an essay.
12. Fraudulent reports **corrupted** the company’s financial record.
13. Biased assumptions **contaminated** the policy analysis.
14. The cheap filler **debased** the original product.
15. Poor translation can **taint** the meaning of a legal document.

**Final memory line:** **Adulterate = make impure by adding bad material = ভেজাল মেশানো**

# 32. Advocate

**Part of speech:** Verb / Noun

**Bangla meaning:** সমর্থন করা, পক্ষ নেওয়া, প্রবক্তা

**Root story / etymology:** From Latin *advocare*: *ad-* = toward + *vocare* = to call. Originally, an advocate was someone “called to help” in court.

**Simple explanation:** To advocate a policy, reform, idea, or right means to support it openly with reasons.

**Memory hook:** **Advocate = a voice called to support.**

**GRE synonyms:** champion, endorse, support, promote, espouse
**Basic synonyms:** back, favor, recommend, argue for
**Antonyms:** oppose, reject, criticize, denounce
**Common usage:** advocate reform, advocate change, advocate for rights, environmental advocate
**Confusing word:** **Advocate ≠ Avocado.** Advocate = সমর্থক; avocado = fruit.

13. The lawyer **argued for** a fairer trial process.
14. The activist **favored** policies that protected workers.
15. The editorial **endorsed** the reform but criticized its weak implementation.

**Final memory line:** **Advocate = support with a voice = সমর্থন করা**

# 33. Aggrandize

**Pronunciation:** uh-GRAN-dyze
**Part of speech:** Verb
**Core meaning:** To increase the power, status, wealth, or importance of someone or something.
**Bangla meaning:** ক্ষমতা/মর্যাদা বাড়ানো, বড় করে দেখানো, প্রভাব বৃদ্ধি করা

**Root story / etymology:** From French/Latin roots related to *grand*, meaning “large” or “great.” Aggrandize literally carries the idea of “making greater.”

**Simple explanation:** To aggrandize means to make something bigger in power, reputation, or importance, sometimes unfairly or selfishly.

**Memory hook:** **Aggrandize = make grand.**

**GRE synonyms:** enlarge, magnify, elevate, augment, enhance
**Basic synonyms:** increase, expand, boost, strengthen
**Antonyms:** diminish, reduce, belittle, weaken
**Common usage:** aggrandize power, aggrandize wealth, self-aggrandizing speech, aggrandize authority
**Confusing word:** **Aggrandize ≠ Grandstand.** Aggrandize = increase power/status; grandstand = show off publicly.

### Examples — 10

1. The ruler tried to aggrandize his power.
2. The businessman used charity to aggrandize his public image.

8. In historical analysis, wars are often linked to rulers’ attempts to aggrandize territory.

3. The award **elevated** her status in the scientific community.
4. Better funding can **augment** research capacity.

8. The discovery **boosted** the researcher’s reputation.

12. The minister tried to **amplify** his public importance.
13. The acquisition **expanded** the firm’s market share.
14. The policy **increased** executive control over local offices.
15. The flattering article **elevated** an ordinary administrator into a heroic figure.

**Final memory line:** **Aggrandize = make greater in power/status = বড় করে তোলা**

# 35. Ambivalent

**Pronunciation:** am-BIV-uh-lənt
**Part of speech:** Adjective
**Core meaning:** Having mixed or conflicting feelings about something.
**Bangla meaning:** দ্বিধাগ্রস্ত, মিশ্র অনুভূতিপূর্ণ, সিদ্ধান্তহীন

**Root story / etymology:** From Latin *ambi-* = both + *valere* = to be strong. Ambivalent means two strong feelings are pulling in different directions.

**Simple explanation:** If you are ambivalent, you do not simply “not care.” You care in two opposite ways.

**Memory hook:** **Ambivalent = both feelings are valid.**

**GRE synonyms:** conflicted, equivocal, uncertain, wavering, torn
**Basic synonyms:** mixed, unsure, undecided, doubtful
**Antonyms:** decisive, certain, sure, resolved
**Common usage:** ambivalent about marriage, ambivalent response, ambivalent attitude, ambivalent feelings
**Confusing word:** **Ambivalent ≠ Indifferent.** Ambivalent = mixed feelings; indifferent = no strong feeling.

### Examples — 10

1. I feel ambivalent about moving abroad.
2. She was ambivalent about accepting the job.
3. He felt ambivalent: excited about success but afraid of pressure.
4. The public remained ambivalent about the new policy.
5. The scientist was ambivalent about the technology because it offered benefits but raised ethical risks.
6. Many students are ambivalent about online learning: it is flexible but isolating.
7. The committee’s ambivalent response showed both hope and concern.
8. In climate policy, governments may be ambivalent about rapid industrial change because it can both create and destroy jobs.
9. The researcher was ambivalent about publishing early results without further validation.
10. The novel’s narrator is ambivalent toward tradition, admiring its stability while criticizing its constraints.

### GRE synonym examples — 15

1. She felt **conflicted** about leaving her family.
2. The voters were **conflicted** about the proposed reform.
3. His answer was **equivocal**, avoiding a clear yes or no.
4. The report offered an **equivocal** conclusion.
5. The committee remained **uncertain** about the risks.
6. He was **wavering** between two career paths.
7. She was **torn** between loyalty and honesty.
8. The evidence left the researchers **unsure**.
9. The minister gave a **doubtful** response to the proposal.
10. The jury remained **undecided** after hours of discussion.
11. The manager’s **mixed** reaction confused the team.
12. His **hesitant** tone revealed inner conflict.
13. The public mood was **divided** after the announcement.
14. The scholar’s assessment was **nuanced**, neither fully approving nor rejecting the theory.
15. The investor’s **wavering** confidence reflected both promise and uncertainty.

**Final memory line:** **Ambivalent = mixed feelings, not no feelings = দ্বিধাগ্রস্ত**

# 41. Banal

**Pronunciation:** buh-NAL / BAY-nəl
**Part of speech:** Adjective
**Core meaning:** Boring because it is too common, ordinary, or unoriginal.
**Bangla meaning:** গতানুগতিক, মামুলি, নীরস, নতুনত্বহীন

**Root story / etymology:** From French *banal*, originally connected with things used commonly by everyone. Later it came to mean “commonplace” and then “boringly unoriginal.”

**Simple explanation:** A banal idea, comment, movie, or essay feels dull because you have heard it many times before.

**Memory hook:** **Banal = boringly normal.**

**GRE synonyms:** trite, hackneyed, commonplace, pedestrian, clichéd
**Basic synonyms:** boring, ordinary, dull, unoriginal
**Antonyms:** original, fresh, novel, imaginative
**Common usage:** banal remark, banal story, banal conclusion, banal observation

**Incredulous ≠ Incredible**
Incredulous = unable to believe.
Incredible = amazing or unbelievable.

**Ingenuous ≠ Ingenious**
Ingenuous = innocent and sincere.
Ingenious = clever and inventive.
